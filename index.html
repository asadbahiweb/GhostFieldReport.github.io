Overview

The GH-01 well in the GHOST field is a dry gas well located in Badin Sindh, Pakistan. The well is situated in sandstone geological formations at a depth of 8540 ft, with an initial reservoir pressure of 4600 psig and a temperature of 170 °F.To estimate the reserves and predict production for the GH-01 well, three different methods were applied volumetric reserve estimation, material balance, and declined curve analysis. Each of these methods has its own advantages and limitations, and by combining the results from multiple methods, we can improve the reliability and precision of our estimates.To apply volumetric reserve estimation to the GH-01 well, we collected geological data on the porosity and thickness of the reservoir rock, as well as data on the gas content of the rock. We used this data to calculate the gas reserves in the well using a volumetric equation. Based on this method, we estimate that the GH-01 well has a total of 1231 Bscf of gas in place.To apply material balance to the GH-01 well, we collected data on the composition of the gas, the reservoir pressure and temperature, and the well characteristics, including the diameter and length of the wellbore, the number and size of perforations, and the skin factor. We used this data to calculate the gas in place and recoverable reserves for the well using a material balance equation. Our analysis indicated that the GH-01 well has a total 1249 bscf gas in place.To apply declined curve analysis to the GH-01 well, we collected production data for the well over a period of time and used this data to fit a decline curve model. The model takes into account the decline in production due to the depletion of the reservoir, as well as any changes in well performance or operating conditions. Based on this model, we estimate that the GH-01 well has the potential to produce a total of 1180 Bscf of gas reserves.Overall, our analysis suggests that the GH-01 well has a significant potential for gas production, with estimated reserves ranging from 1180 Bscf to 1246 Bscf, depending on the method used. However, it is important to note that these estimates are subject to various assumptions and limitations, including the quality of the data and the accuracy of the models applied. For example,the declined curve analysis assumes that the decline in production follows a predictable pattern, the material balance requires detailed and accurate data on the reservoir and well characteristics, and the volumetric reserve estimation assumes that the reservoir rock has a uniform porosity and gas content.

The Ghost gas field is located in Badin district of Sindh province, Pakistan and was discovered in 1999. This field is an example of a high-pressure, subsurface rock formation that contains natural gas, located in a sandstone geologic formation at a depth of 8,540 ft. The Ghost gas field's production began in 2001, utilizing modern drilling techniques to extract the natural gas from the reservoir. The field is notable for being a significant source of energy, used for heating and power generation, as well as for transportation.

Due to high pressure, special care has to be taken while drilling, completing and operating the wells in the field. Also, advanced production techniques may be required to handle the high pressure and bring the gas to the surface. The reservoir contains a large volume of natural gas that is extracted through the wells drilled into the formation.

The Ghost gas field has been a major contributor to the energy sector of Pakistan since its production started. The field's exploration and development history is a testament to the advances in technology and the expertise of the operators. The Ghost gas field is owned and operated by a National Oil and Gas company of Pakistan. With the continued efforts of the operator, the field is expected to continue production and provide a steady supply of natural gas for many years to come.

This method is based on the assumption that the reservoir rock has a uniform porosity and gas content, which may not always be the case in practice. However, it has the advantage of being relatively simple and easy to apply, and can be used to estimate reserves for both volatile and non-volatile reservoirs.

Material Balance

Material balance analysis is a technique used to estimate the recoverable reserves of a well by analyzing data on the composition of the gas, reservoir pressure and temperature, and well characteristics. It is based on the principles of mass and energy conservation, and uses PVT data, reservoir data, fluid properties, and well production data to estimate the original oil and gas in place, and the amounts that will be produced over time. It can be useful for estimating reserves for both volatile and non-volatile reservoirs, but requires detailed and accurate data on the reservoir and well characteristics.

Decline Curve Analysis (DCA)

Declined curve analysis involves using production data from the well to model the decline in production over time and estimate the ultimate recovery of the well. This method is widely used in the oil and gas industry and has the advantage of being based on actual production data, which can be more reliable than estimates based on geology or reservoir characteristics. However, it relies on the assumption that the decline in production follows a predictable pattern, which may not always be the case in practice. By analyzing the decline rate and initial production rate, the decline curve analysis allows the estimation of future production rate. A decline curve model was fitted using production data collected for the well over a period of time. The model takes into account the decline in production due to the depletion of the reservoir, as well as any changes in well performance or operating conditions.
